There is an area of mathematics known as game theory. Game theory (the work of John Nash in particular) is used to model what agents do, or should do, when they compete.

In game theory we carefully define games, and assign payouts to 'plays' which consist of a strategy choice for each player. There is a classic game, known as the prisoner's dilemma. The prisoner's dilemma describes a race to the bottom. This model is used to talk about competition between firms and social groups, as well as individuals. The example that follows is a simplification, but the conclusions are valid.

Let's say that two players are enemies, and let's assume for now that they don't care about each other. Each can choose to either buy a gun or not buy a gun for their fight. The numbers in the pic related are utility values as payouts for that play, for each player. In each pair, the first number represents the utility to player one, and the second number represents the utility to player two.

Now, if neither player buys a gun, they are evenly matched, we assign a utility of 2 for the payout for each player. If one player buys a gun and the other does not, the gun owner wins 3-0. (Why not 4-0? That is next). If they both buy guns however, they are still evenly matched, but the fight is more dangerous, and their homes now have guns in them, which statistically correlates to more gun injuries, so they each lose one utility.
